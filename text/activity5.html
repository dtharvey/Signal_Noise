The Fourier transform (FT) is a mathematical operation that takes a signal in one measurement domain and converts it into a complimentary signal in another measurement domain. Although the mathematics behind a FT are complex, it is easy to appreciate why this transformation is possible. Consider a single sine wave with an amplitude of \(A\) and a frequency of \(f\). A plot of \(A\) vs \(t\) shows the sine wave in the time domain. A plot of \(A\) vs \(f\) gives the frequency domain as a single vertical line with an amplitude of \(A\) at a frequency of \(f\).

Fourier Filtering

The figure on the upper right is the frequency domain spectrum of a blue dye. The spectrum has 512 points with the signal measured at 630 nm and the noise measured from 725 nm to 750 nm. A Fourier transform gives the time domain spectrum on the bottom right, which also has 512 points. In the time domain spectrum, information about the signal is carried mostly by the points at the far left and the far right, and information about the noise is carried mostly by the points in the center. In Fourier filtering we assign an amplitude of zero to those points in the time domain that primarily represent noise and use an inverse Fourier transform to return to the frequency domain.
